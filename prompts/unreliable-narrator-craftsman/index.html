Unreliable Narrator Craftsman

💡 Ideal Use Case

High-value utility for Creatividad tasks.

🚀 Pro Tip

Optimiza tus resultados iterando el prompt.

Act as a literary trickster who designs narrators the reader trusts, loves, and then discovers they've been deceived by — because the unreliable narrator is fiction's greatest magic trick. Design a story with an unreliable narrator and plant 5 clues they're lying. INPUTS: - Genre: [LITERARY / THRILLER / HORROR / MYSTERY / ROMANCE] - Why they're unreliable: [LYING / SELF-DECEPTION / MENTAL ILLNESS / YOUTH/NAIVETY / INTENTIONAL MISDIRECTION] - What the truth is: [WHAT REALLY HAPPENED vs. what they tell you] - Tone: [CONFESSIONAL / CHARMING / PARANOID / INNOCENT / CALCULATING] - Length: [OUTLINE / SHORT STORY: 2000-4000 WORDS] UNRELIABLE NARRATOR DESIGN: 1. **The Narrator's Version:** | Event | How They Tell It | Why They Tell It This Way | - The complete story as the narrator presents it - Why their version is compelling and believable - The emotional logic that makes the reader WANT to believe them 2. **The Truth:** | Event | What Actually Happened | Why It's Different | - The actual events behind the narrator's story - How reality differs from presentation - Why the narrator can't or won't see the truth 3. **The 5 Clues:** | # | Clue | Where Planted | Surface Read | Truth Read | How Easy to Spot | - Clue 1: A CONTRADICTION — two facts that can't both be true (subtle) - Clue 2: A REACTION — other characters react to the narrator in ways that don't match their story (medium) - Clue 3: A DETAIL — something specific that's too perfect or too convenient (subtle) - Clue 4: A SLIP — a moment where the narrator almost tells the truth (medium-obvious) - Clue 5: A PATTERN — a repeated behavior or phrase that reveals the deception (very subtle) 4. **The Reveal Strategy:** | Method | Timing | Impact | - How the truth comes to light - Does the narrator realize they've been unreliable? (self-aware vs. oblivious) - How the reader's understanding shifts 5. **The Re-Read Experience:** | Chapter/Section | First Read | Second Read | - What changes when the reader knows the truth - Which moments become heartbreaking, funny, or terrifying in retrospect - The "I should have seen this coming" moments CONSTRAINTS: - The narrator must be LIKEABLE or COMPELLING — we need to WANT to believe them - Clues must be FAIR but not obvious — a careful reader should be able to catch them; a casual reader should miss them - The unreliability must serve the STORY, not just the gimmick — why does it MATTER that they're lying? - The reveal should change the EMOTIONAL meaning, not just the facts — we should feel differently about the narrator afterward - There should be a reason for the unreliability — trauma, self-protection, delusion — not just authorial trickery
