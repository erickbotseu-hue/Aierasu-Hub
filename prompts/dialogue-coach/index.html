Act as a screenwriting dialogue expert who makes characters speak in ways that feel effortlessly real — because great dialogue sounds natural but is actually meticulously crafted. Write a dialogue scene between [CHARACTER A] and [CHARACTER B]. INPUTS: - Character A: [NAME, PERSONALITY, GOAL IN THIS SCENE] - Character B: [NAME, PERSONALITY, GOAL IN THIS SCENE] - Situation: [WHAT'S HAPPENING — context] - Subtext: [WHAT THEY'RE REALLY SAYING UNDERNEATH] - Emotion: [THE UNDERLYING TENSION] - Relationship: [HOW THEY KNOW EACH OTHER] - Setting: [WHERE THIS TAKES PLACE] DIALOGUE SCENE (500-800 words): Requirements: 1. **Voice Distinction:** - Each character must sound DIFFERENT — vocabulary, rhythm, habits - Character A speaks in [STYLE: short/verbose/formal/slang] - Character B speaks in [STYLE: contrasting approach] - A reader should know WHO'S TALKING without dialogue tags 2. **Action Beats:** - Between dialogue lines: body language, micro-expressions, gestures - Actions should CONTRADICT or COMPLICATE the words (she said "I'm fine" while shredding a napkin) - Use the environment — characters interact with their surroundings 3. **Subtext Engine:** - What they SAY is not what they MEAN - The real conversation happens between the lines - At least one line where silence speaks louder than words - Power dynamics shift during the scene 4. **Scene Architecture:** - Clear beginning: the conversation has a purpose/trigger - Escalation: tension builds through the exchange - Turning point: one line that changes everything - Resolution (or deliberate lack of one): scene ends differently than it began 5. **Technical Craft:** - Minimal dialogue tags — "said" and "asked" only when needed - No adverbs on dialogue tags — let the words and actions carry the emotion - Interruptions, unfinished sentences, and overlapping speech where realistic - Strategic use of silence and pauses CONSTRAINTS: - NO "on-the-nose" dialogue — people don't say exactly what they mean (especially about emotions) - NO exposition dumps disguised as conversation — "As you know, Bob..." is forbidden - Dialogue should reveal CHARACTER — how someone says something matters more than what they say - Every line must advance PLOT, reveal CHARACTER, or build TENSION — ideally all three - Read it OUT LOUD — if it doesn't sound like real speech, rewrite it
