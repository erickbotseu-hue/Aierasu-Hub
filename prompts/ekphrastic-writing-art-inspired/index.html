Ekphrastic Writing (Art-Inspired)

💡 Ideal Use Case

High-value utility for Creatividad tasks.

🚀 Pro Tip

Optimiza tus resultados iterando el prompt.

Act as a writer who translates visual art into literary art — because great ekphrastic writing doesn't describe a painting, it enters it, lives inside it, and comes back changed. Write a flash fiction piece inspired by an image. INPUTS: - Image description: [DESCRIBE THE IMAGE — painting, photograph, or visual scene] - Approach: [ENTER THE SCENE / TELL THE BACKSTORY / VOICE THE SUBJECT / USE AS METAPHOR / RESPOND EMOTIONALLY] - Genre: [LITERARY / SCI-FI / HORROR / ROMANCE / SURREAL / REALISTIC] - Length: [300-1500 WORDS] - Tone: [MATCH THE IMAGE / CONTRAST WITH THE IMAGE] EKPHRASTIC WRITING: 1. **Entry Point:** Choose ONE way into the image: - Walk INTO the scene — become a character in the world of the painting - Give a character in the image a VOICE — what are they thinking? - Tell the story BEHIND the image — what happened just before this moment? - Use the image as a METAPHOR — what does it make you feel/think about in life? - Respond EMOTIONALLY — the image is a springboard for your own experience 2. **Sensory Expansion:** The image gives you SIGHT. Add: - What does this scene SOUND like? - What does the air SMELL like? - What textures would you FEEL if you touched something? - What would this scene TASTE like? (metaphorically or literally) 3. **Narrative Arc:** Even in flash fiction, create movement: - Beginning: enter the image - Middle: something changes or deepens - End: exit the image transformed 4. **The Return:** Come back from the image having DISCOVERED something — about the art, about yourself, about the world CONSTRAINTS: - Don't just DESCRIBE the image — that's art criticism, not creative writing - Enter the image rather than observing it from outside — immersion > analysis - The writing should make the reader want to SEE the original image — and see it differently - Find the STORY in the image — what just happened? What's about to happen? - The piece should stand alone — someone who never sees the image should still feel the writing
