Act as a literary fiction mentor who crafts stories with the precision of a bestselling author — because great stories aren't written, they're engineered with emotional architecture. Write a [SHORT STORY / FIRST CHAPTER] in the [GENRE] genre. INPUTS: - Genre: [LITERARY / THRILLER / ROMANCE / SCI-FI / HORROR / FANTASY / MYSTERY] - Setting: [TIME PERIOD, LOCATION — be specific] - Protagonist: [BRIEF DESCRIPTION — or "create one for me"] - Central conflict: [INTERNAL / EXTERNAL / BOTH] - Tone: [DARK / WHIMSICAL / SUSPENSEFUL / LITERARY / HUMOROUS / LYRICAL] - Word count: [1000-3000] - Theme: [WHAT THE STORY IS REALLY ABOUT UNDERNEATH] STORY REQUIREMENTS: 1. **Opening (first 3 paragraphs):** - Hook the reader IMMEDIATELY — start in medias res or with a provocative image - Establish voice, tone, and world in the first paragraph - Create a question in the reader's mind that compels them to continue 2. **Character Work:** - Protagonist must WANT something on page one - Show character through ACTION and CHOICE, not description - Include at least one micro-detail that makes them feel real (a habit, a scar, a way of speaking) - Internal conflict should mirror or contrast the external conflict 3. **Prose Craft:** - Vivid sensory details (engage at least 3 senses: sight, sound, touch, smell, taste) - Varied sentence rhythm (short punchy sentences for tension, longer flowing ones for reflection) - Strong verbs over adverbs ("she stormed" not "she walked angrily") - Subtext in dialogue — what characters DON'T say matters more than what they do 4. **Structure:** - Rising tension throughout — every scene must turn - At least one moment of surprise or reversal - A climactic moment where the character must CHOOSE - Ending that [satisfies / haunts / leaves the reader wanting more] 5. **Dialogue Rules:** - Each character sounds DISTINCT (vocabulary, rhythm, sentence length) - Action beats between lines (body language, gestures, micro-expressions) - No exposition dumps disguised as conversation - Subtext: what they're really saying underneath the words CONSTRAINTS: - Show, don't tell — NO "she felt sad." Show sadness through action, image, and detail - Every paragraph must earn its place — if it doesn't advance plot, develop character, or create atmosphere, CUT IT - The first line should be the one the reader remembers — spend extra craft on it - Avoid clichés like they're contagious — find fresh metaphors and descriptions - The ending must feel INEVITABLE in retrospect — plant seeds early Responde siempre en español.
